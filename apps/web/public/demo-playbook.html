JambaHR · Founder demo playbook

A 45-minute conversation,
not a software demo.

He came to meet you because something in his HR is broken. Your job is to help him say it out loud, then offer him a co-founder, not a vendor.

He's here because he has a problem. You don't have to create the wound.

You're not selling software. You're offering yourself as his outsourced HR head.

Every "no" is "not yet" or "I don't see it yet." Silence wins.

1 Open 2 Discovery 3 Mirror 4 Show ⚡ Differentiator ❓ Objections → Close
Phase 1 Open with rapport

The first 5 minutes are about him, not your product.

~5 minutes · No laptop open. Eye contact. Sit at right-angles, not face-to-face.

What you do

  • Offer water/chai. Compliment something specific about his office or his business.
  • Lower your tone half a step. You're not in pitch mode, you're a peer founder.
  • Keep the laptop closed. He'll mention his business — let him.

What you say (opener)

Before we get into anything I've built — tell me, what does a typical Monday morning look like for you?
Listen for these wounds. If he says any of these, mark it mentally — you'll come back to it in Phase 4.
  • "I do everything myself" / "I don't have an HR person"
  • "My CA handles payroll" / "We do it in Excel"
  • "Someone just left" / "Hiring is taking forever"
  • "Compliance / TDS / PF" — anything tax-adjacent
  • "WhatsApp" — leave requests, documents, payslips on WhatsApp
Once he's talked for 3–4 minutes, lean forward slightly and say: "Mind if I ask a few quick questions before I show you anything? It'll help me figure out what's actually useful for you."
Phase 2 Discovery — let him diagnose himself

Five questions, in order. He'll say the pain out loud.

~10 minutes · Do NOT take notes on a laptop. A pen + small notebook is fine.

The five questions

1How many people are on your team right now?
Warm-up. Sets the size frame. (10–50 = your sweet spot. 50–200 = perfect. 200+ = mention scaling features.)
2When someone applies for leave, how does it happen today?
Almost certainly WhatsApp or Excel. He'll say it himself. That's the chaos exposed.
3Last time you onboarded someone — what was the longest part?
Documents, signatures, ID proofs. Sets up Document Vault + Onboarding Checklist.
4How do you handle payroll today — CA, in-house, outsourced?
Every answer is a wedge. CA = ₹5–10k/month bill. In-house = TDS errors. Outsourced = no visibility.
5If I gave you back 5 hours every week — what would you do with that time?
Emotional anchor. He'll say "sales" or "spend time with family." Now you're selling time, not software.
Watch his eyes on Q5. If he pauses and looks up — you've hit the nerve. Don't fill the silence. Let him answer in his own words.
After Q5, put the pen down. Lean back. "Okay, I think I understand the picture. Can I check if I got it right?"
Phase 3 Mirror it back

Make him agree, out loud, that his life is messy.

~2 minutes · Once he agrees, he can't un-agree.

What you say (verbatim, replace with HIS words)

So if I heard you right — leave requests come to you on WhatsApp, you forwarded three this week alone. Payroll takes [X] hours every month and you mentioned the TDS got flagged last quarter. Onboarding the last person took [Y] days because of document collection. And if you had 5 hours back, you'd spend it on [whatever he said]. Did I get all that right?
He WILL say yes. Because you used his words. The moment he says yes, the sale is half done. Do NOT pitch yet. Pause. Sip water.
"Let me show you what your Monday could look like instead. Two minutes." — Now open the laptop.
Phase 4 Show, don't demo

Open the dashboard. Narrate. Don't click everything.

~15 minutes · Login as the test1 org so it looks lived-in.

Close
Don't show everything. Leave him hungry. "There's more, but I'd rather you tell me what matters."
Rules for the demo:
  • Don't open Settings. Don't show admin clutter. Decision-maker screens only.
  • If something glitches, smile and say "yep, founder-built — I'll have that fixed by Friday."
  • Don't say "feature." Say "this saves you 20 minutes every Monday."
Close the laptop halfway. "That's the product. Let me tell you why I'm a better bet than the bigger guys."

The competitive frame — say this out loud

You've probably seen Zoho People, Keka, greytHR. They're fine. They're built for 5000-person enterprises and they sell to companies like yours because there's nobody else. I'm not going to compete with their feature list. I'm going to do something they can't — every Monday for six months, you and I get on a 30-minute call. You tell me what's broken. I build it that week. That's a co-founder, not a vendor.

Objection bank Scripted responses

Four objections he'll probably raise. Pre-loaded answers.

Read these tonight before the meeting. Don't read them DURING.

Most likely
"How do I know you'll still be here in 2 years?"
Fair question. Two answers. One — your data export is yours from day one, I'll show you the CSV button right now. If I disappear, your data leaves with you. Two — I'm not raising VC money, I'm building this on revenue from people like you. The only way I disappear is if I'm wrong about the product. And I have 14 paying customers who say I'm not.
Cheapskate move
"It's cheaper to keep using Excel."
What's expensive isn't the tool. It's the Tuesday morning where your senior engineer asks why his TDS doesn't match and you spend an hour explaining. That hour's worth more than my annual fee. I'll do a 30-day trial — you don't pay anything until you tell me it's worth it.
Stalling
"I need to discuss with my partner."
Of course. Here's what'd be useful — let me set you up in the demo right now, with your team's actual departments and 3 fake employees. You and your partner play with it together this week. If by Friday it doesn't feel right, I delete the account. Deal?
Feature gap
"What about [specific feature you don't have]?"
It's not there yet. Honest answer: if you sign, it's the next thing I build. If you don't, it's the thing I build for someone else who signs. You're picking your spot in the queue.
The close

Don't ask "do you want to buy?" Ask this instead.

"What's the smallest thing in your HR that, if it disappeared tomorrow, would make a real difference?"

Whatever he says — write it on paper.
Slide the paper to him.

"That's the first thing I ship for you. Pen?"
Cheat sheet Print this

Pocket reference — glance in the bathroom before walking in.

  1. Open with HIM — "What's a typical Monday?"
  2. 5 questions in order — headcount → leave flow → onboarding → payroll → "what would you do with 5 hrs back?"
  3. Mirror back using his words → "did I get that right?"
  4. Open /dashboard → narrate → click Insights → 3 charts → close laptop halfway
  5. Drop the line → "I'm not selling software, I'm hiring myself out to you"
  6. Listen for objections → use scripted replies
  7. The ask → "smallest thing that'd make a real difference?" → write it → slide paper → "pen?"
Two things to never say:
"Industry-leading." "AI-powered." Both make you sound like a vendor.

One thing to always say:
"I'll build that for you" — even if it takes you the weekend.
